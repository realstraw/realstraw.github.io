I was in a meeting not long ago where everyone agreed on a decision. Heads
nodding, no objections, clear next steps. A few weeks later, the implementation
looked nothing like what anyone had intended. What happened? We had agreement,
but we didn't have alignment. They're not the same thing, and confusing the two
is one of the most common and costly breakdowns I see in organizations.

Think about any conversation where you and someone else land on the same
conclusion. It feels good -- you're on the same page, right? But are you
really? There are actually several different states you could be in:

**1. We agree on both the what and the why.**

This is true alignment. We want the same outcome, and we want it for the same
reasons. When this is the case, the downstream decisions -- the how, the
tradeoffs, the priorities -- tend to take care of themselves. People can
independently make good judgment calls because they share the same foundation.
Reed Hastings and Erin Meyer explore this idea at length in [_No Rules Rules:
Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention_](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/49099937-no-rules-rules) -- Netflix's culture of freedom and
responsibility only works because people are deeply aligned on the why, which
lets them make independent decisions without needing top-down approval.

**2. We're just talking past each other.**

Sometimes there's no real agreement at all -- not on the outcome, not on the
reasoning. We're just using the same words to mean different things. This is
more common than people think, especially across teams or levels of an
organization. People leave the conversation thinking they had a productive
discussion, but they were never actually in the same conversation to begin with.
There's nothing to fix here except to slow down and make sure you're actually
talking about the same thing before debating what to do about it.

**3. We agree on the outcome but have different reasons.**

This is where things get tricky. On the surface, everything looks fine. We
agreed, we shook hands, we moved forward. But because our underlying reasons
differ, the moment we hit a fork in the road -- and we always do -- we'll
disagree on which way to go. The agreement was shallow. It doesn't survive
contact with reality. And over time, this shallow agreement erodes. Each
implementation decision drifts a little further from the original intent until
eventually, people are talking past each other entirely.

**4. We agree on the reason but have different conclusions.**

This is actually a much healthier place to be than it sounds. When people share
the same why but arrive at different conclusions, you have the ingredients for a
genuinely productive discussion. The shared reasoning gives you common ground to
evaluate the options. You can debate the what on its merits, grounded in a
shared understanding of what matters. From here, advancing toward true alignment
is natural.

Of these four states, the why is what matters most. Only when we are aligned on
the why can we start having truly good discussions about the outcome and the
how.

I've seen this play out many times: leadership agrees on a direction, issues a
mandate, and then watches the implementation produce the exact opposite of what
they intended. How does that happen? Because the people doing the implementing
agreed on the what -- they heard the mandate -- but never internalized the why.
Without the why, every implementation decision becomes a coin flip. And enough
coin flips in the wrong direction will invert the original intent entirely. The
alignment was never there; only the agreement was.

The fix isn't to mandate harder or to write longer strategy documents. The fix
is to communicate the why, not the what. Say leadership decides to consolidate
from three internal tools into one. The mandate is clear: "We're moving to a
single platform." But why? Is it to reduce maintenance cost? To improve the user
experience? To unblock a future migration? If the team thinks it's about cost
but leadership cares about user experience, the team will optimize for the
cheapest integration -- and leadership will wonder why the result feels worse
than what they had before.

So talk about why we're doing this. Talk about what problem we're solving and
for whom. Then, continuously align on the why as the work progresses. Use the
why to evaluate the what -- every proposal, every design decision, every
tradeoff should be held up against the shared reasoning. If a decision doesn't
serve the why, it doesn't matter how reasonable it looks on its own.

Agreement is easy. Alignment is work. But alignment is the only kind of
agreement that compounds.
